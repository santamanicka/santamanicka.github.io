About

Understanding what biological systems compute and how.

Non-neural biological systems routinely solve problems that look cognitive: a developing embryo coordinates millions of cells into a body plan, a gene regulatory network forms and recalls associative memories, an embryonic collective communicates to mount a rescue response under duress. We know a great deal about the molecular parts. What we lack is an understanding of what these systems are actually computing — what is represented, how information flows across space and time, where the decision boundaries lie, and how the outcome stays robust.

The interpretability gap
Minimality as interpretability strategy

That is the problem I work on. My approach has two steps. First, I build deliberately minimal models — stripped to the fewest components that still produce the behavior of interest. Minimality is not an aesthetic choice; it is a strategy for understanding. A model with two essential variables can be fully understood; a model with two hundred cannot.

Second, I develop and apply analytical methods — spatiotemporal sensitivity fields, causal network decompositions, information-theoretic measures — that extract the computational and cognitive structure hidden in these models. In a 2019 framework paper (The Cognitive Lens), I laid out this program: treat biological patterning as information processing, then use integration methods to read out what the system represents, when it shifts from being externally driven to self-maintaining, and which cells integrate information from which others. The goal is not just to make models interpretable, but to discover whether cognition-like processes — decision-making, memory, communication — are genuinely at work in non-neural biology.

The Cognitive Lens framework

Before Tufts, I studied adaptive agents at Sussex and Boolean network dynamics at Indiana. Across all three settings, the question has been the same: what and how do collectives of simple components compute — and can we ground these complex phenomena in fundamental physical principles?

Research

Each project builds a minimal model of a biological phenomenon, then uses the cognitive lens to ask: what is this system computing?

How electric fields might coordinate collective cell decisions

Cells in a tissue are coupled by gap junctions and bathed in a self-generated electric field. I showed that when ion channel conductances are modulated by this field — a stigmergic loop where each cell modifies the shared medium it also reads — a lattice of identical cells can spontaneously break symmetry and produce face-like voltage pre-patterns. Jacobian and Hessian time-series analysis reveals that the field acts as a single low-dimensional control parameter, catalyzing long-range causal interactions among cells that have no direct knowledge of one another. The field is the medium through which the tissue computes a global plan from purely local rules.

Manicka & Levin, 2025 · Manicka & Levin, 2019 · Manicka, Pai & Levin, 2023

How bioelectric tissues might compute without a brain

Non-neural cells can compute. I showed that a small bioelectric network — cells coupled by gap junctions — can implement AND, OR, and NOT logic gates through learned ion channel conductance parameters acting as weights and biases. The inputs are transient: set externally at discrete moments, then removed. Yet the output persists. The network dynamically fixes the input state after each stimulus — a form of memory that emerges from the learned ion-channel dynamics, not from any dedicated memory structure. Computation and memory co-emerge from the same biophysics. But this memory is implicit and fragile. What makes biological memory robust — durable enough to anchor a cell fate across a lifetime?

Manicka & Levin, 2019

How inter-embryonic communication might create collective memories

A voltage pattern may last seconds; a cell fate lasts a lifetime. I model the cascade from membrane voltage through calcium influx to CaMKII activation, where competitive self-reinforcement creates a bistable switch: cells that saw high calcium lock ON, cells that didn't lock OFF, and the pattern persists after the original signal is gone. This persistent memory then enables something unexpected: when embryos are stressed, CaMKII-derived signals produce a diffusive stress field. A single embryo's signal is too weak to trigger repair — the self-cure paradox. But when multiple stressed embryos share a medium, their fields sum and cross a collective threshold, triggering group rescue. Memory makes computation durable; collective communication makes it powerful.

Manicka & Levin, 2025 · Biswas, Manicka, Hoel & Levin, 2021 · collective rescue: ongoing

How gene networks might use attention to compute

A gene with eight regulators may in practice be controlled by two — the rest are buffered by the logic of the regulatory function. I developed measures that expose this hidden simplicity: effective connectivity (Keff), which counts the inputs that truly matter, and unified canalization (Ku), which exploits permutation symmetry among interchangeable inputs. Applied to 70+ published biological networks, these measures explain why living systems sit near dynamical criticality even when their wiring diagrams predict chaos. Decomposing Boolean functions into their Taylor series further stratifies how a network processes information: which contributions are linear, which are pairwise, and which are irreducibly higher-order.

Manicka, Marques-Pita & Rocha, 2022 · Manicka, Johnson, Levin & Murrugarra, 2023 · Manicka, 2017 (dissertation)

How multicellular patterning networks might leverage coarse-grained causation

A ring of twelve cells, each containing an identical local gene network, can learn to maintain and regenerate a spatial pattern — if you let the inner network tune the outer network's weights. I use machine learning to discover these two-level architectures, then causal-network analysis to interpret what the learned system is actually doing: breaking symmetry, establishing long-range influence, and partitioning into modular control domains. The point is not just that the system works, but that it is small enough to fully explain — a proof of sufficiency where every mechanism earns its place.

Manicka & Levin, 2022 · Manicka, Hammelman, Siegelmann & Levin, 2019 · Kudithipudi, ..., Manicka, ..., Siegelmann, 2022

How minimal adaptive agents cognize in the dynamic state space

Before working on tissues, I asked the same question about minimal adaptive agents: given a system with only a handful of neurons and a body, what is it actually computing? At Sussex I built the simplest agent that could sustain itself in a continuous environment, then subjected it to an exhaustive dynamical 'psychoanalysis' — mapping how internal states, sensorimotor coupling, and environmental feedback jointly produce cognition-like behavior. In follow-up work, I studied how evolved agents ground referential signals in shared sensorimotor experience, and how physical spring networks achieve ultrastability through local homeostatic rules. In each case: build the simplest possible system, then uncover the computation it performs.
